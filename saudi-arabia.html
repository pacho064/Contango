Output decline and the Yanbu reroute

Saudi Arabia entered the crisis with 10.2M bbl/d of production — near its operational ceiling. The Hormuz closure immediately created an export bottleneck: tankers could not safely load at Ras Tanura or Jubail. Saudi Aramco responded within hours of the conflict escalation, rerouting crude through the East-West Pipeline (Petroline) to Yanbu on the Red Sea.

The response was faster and more effective than any previous test of the contingency. Aramco CEO Amin Nasser confirmed on March 10 that the pipeline would reach its 7M bbl/d maximum capacity within days. Bloomberg confirmed it hit that mark on March 28. But the bypass has a ceiling — and Saudi Arabia was already hitting it.

Production was cut by roughly 2M bbl/d in the first weeks of the crisis, not because of infrastructure damage, but because the pipeline could not absorb everything the Eastern Province fields were producing. The fields not connected to the pipeline — including some Arab Heavy and Arab Medium production — were shut in.

Crisis narrative · February–June 2026

The bypass that partially worked — and what it cost

When the US and Israel struck Iran on February 28, Saudi Arabia faced the scenario it had prepared for since the 1980s Iran-Iraq war: Hormuz denial. The East-West Pipeline — built precisely for this contingency — was activated at maximum urgency.

Aramco CEO Amin Nasser · March 10 2026

"This is the biggest crisis the region's oil and gas industry has faced."

The pipeline is a logistical triumph and a geographic rerouting of risk. Saudi Arabia traded the certainty of Iranian proximity in the Gulf for exposure to Houthi and Iranian proxy forces in the Red Sea. Yanbu, which sits on the Red Sea coast, must still route tankers through the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — another chokepoint. Attacks on energy facilities in Yanbu, including the Samref refinery, have already occurred.

The second structural constraint: five Red Sea coast refineries absorb approximately 1.8M bbl/d of pipeline throughput before a single export barrel moves. With the pipeline at 7M bbl/d and 1.8M consumed domestically, the effective export window through Yanbu is roughly 5.2M bbl/d — well below Saudi Arabia's pre-crisis 7M bbl/d export rate through Hormuz.

A third problem emerged in April and May: Arab buyers stopped buying. The Dubai backwardation structure — where near-term cargoes command extreme premiums — caused Aramco's OSP formula to mechanically generate differentials that made Saudi crude commercially unworkable for Asian refiners. The Dubai M1-M3 spread averaged $37/bbl in March, compressing to ~$13 in April and ~$8 in May. As spreads normalise, OSPs are expected to return to workable levels — but Saudi Arabia lost weeks of sales volume it cannot recover.

Who buys Saudi crude — and who lost supply

Saudi Arabia exports approximately 7M bbl/d in normal conditions. The Hormuz crisis disrupted all Gulf-routed flows. Yanbu now exports ~3.66M bbl/d — about half the pre-crisis level — primarily Arab Light to Asian buyers willing to pay elevated war premiums.
