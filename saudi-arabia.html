Breaking · April 8–9 2026

The bypass was hit — Petroline pumping station struck hours after ceasefire

Hours after the US-Iran ceasefire was announced on April 8, a pumping station on Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline was struck in a drone attack. Saudi state news agency SPA confirmed the damage on April 9, citing the Ministry of Energy. Throughput on the pipeline fell by approximately 700,000 barrels per day.

The same wave of strikes hit the Manifa offshore oilfield (−300,000 bpd) and the Khurais onshore field (−300,000 bpd), bringing total Saudi production capacity loss to approximately 600,000 bpd. The Ras Tanura refinery, SATORP in Jubail, SAMREF in Yanbu, and the Riyadh refinery were also targeted. Ju'aymah LPG and NGL processing facilities were struck by fires affecting LPG and gas liquids exports.

The analysis on this page was correct — and confirmed by events
The Yanbu bottleneck section below was written in March 2026, before the pipeline was hit. The argument: the East-West pipeline bypass was structurally insufficient and physically vulnerable. On April 8 that vulnerability was directly exploited. The bypass capacity Saudi Arabia spent weeks building — routing 5M+ bbl/d via Petroline to Yanbu — was degraded by 700,000 bpd in a single strike on one pumping station.

Saudi Arabia announced partial restoration of pipeline capacity and Manifa production on April 12. The timeline for full restoration of refinery capacity at Ras Tanura, SATORP, and SAMREF is not yet confirmed. The combined effect: Saudi Arabia's Gulf export route is blocked (Hormuz), its Red Sea bypass has been degraded, two major oilfields are at reduced capacity, and four refineries have been struck.

Partial recovery announced — April 12
Saudi Arabia said it restored full pumping capacity through the East-West pipeline and output from the Manifa field by April 12. Refinery restoration timelines at SATORP Jubail, Ras Tanura, SAMREF and Riyadh remain unconfirmed. JPMorgan analysts described the damage as a "measurable supply shock" equivalent to roughly 10% of pre-conflict Saudi crude export capacity.

Infrastructure constraint — written March 2026, confirmed April 2026

The Yanbu bottleneck — why the Red Sea bypass fails at scale

Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (Petroline) runs 1,200 kilometres from Abqaiq in the Eastern Province to Yanbu on the Red Sea. It is routinely cited as the strategic answer to Strait of Hormuz vulnerability. The argument is simple: if Hormuz is disrupted, redirect exports westward through the pipeline to Yanbu and out via the Red Sea.

The argument has a structural flaw. The pipeline can carry up to 7 million barrels per day. Yanbu's export terminal capacity is approximately 1.8 million barrels per day. The pipeline is not the bottleneck. The port is.

The bypass capacity problem
Even at full pipeline utilisation, Saudi Arabia can only bypass Hormuz for ~18% of its daily exports through Yanbu. The remaining ~8.4 million barrels per day remain Hormuz-dependent. No announced investment programme exists to expand Yanbu's export capacity. And as of April 8, the pipeline itself is no longer intact.

The second structural problem is Bab el-Mandeb. The pipeline bypass trades one chokepoint for another. Saudi crude routed via Yanbu must still transit this strait — and the Houthi threat to Bab el-Mandeb remains an active strategic option coordinated with Tehran.

By mid-March 2026, approximately 30 supertankers had rerouted from Hormuz to Yanbu, far exceeding the usual two per month. Yanbu hit a record 5.9M bbl/d export rate on March 9. Every tanker departing Yanbu must still pass through Bab el-Mandeb to reach Asian buyers.

David Butter, associate fellow at Chatham House: "Even at full capacity these routes can only cover about one-quarter of the oil that normally goes through the Strait of Hormuz." The East-West pipeline and UAE's Habshan-Fujairah pipeline combined. Not Saudi Arabia's bypass alone.

Active threat — April 2026

Bab el-Mandeb: the second chokepoint remains in play

The Houthis have not entered the US-Iran war directly, but the conditions under which they would remain public and specific. Houthi political bureau member Mohammed al-Bukhaiti stated on 19 March 2026 that the group is actively examining closure of Bab el-Mandeb to vessels from nations engaged in hostilities against Iran — and that any decision would be coordinated with Tehran.

This matters for Saudi Arabia specifically because of route dependency. About 75% of Saudi Arabia's crude exports go to Asian markets — China, India, Japan, South Korea — meaning tankers must transit Bab el-Mandeb to deliver crude loaded at Yanbu. A Houthi blockade would eliminate the only viable bypass of Hormuz that exists at scale.

Strategic pause, not incapacity
The current absence of Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping reflects deliberate strategic timing. Analysts at the International Crisis Group, RUSI, and Global Security Review assess the group is calibrating its entry — waiting for Iranian coordination rather than acting independently. The ceasefire fragility and ongoing Israeli attacks in Lebanon keep this risk live.
WTO/AXSMarine · Hormuz Trade Tracker · 24 March 2026
AIS-tracked outbound crude shipments from the Persian Gulf fell to near zero on 28 February. LNG and fertilizer shipments dropped simultaneously. The tracker — a joint project of AXSMarine (Signal Group) and the WTO — confirms complete effective closure of outbound maritime trade through Hormuz, corroborating the route-shift pressure on Yanbu.

Infrastructure vulnerability

Abqaiq — single point of failure for global supply

Saudi Arabia's oil processing infrastructure is extraordinarily concentrated. The Abqaiq facility in the Eastern Province handles the preparation of crude for export — removing hydrogen sulphide from sour crude, adjusting API gravity, and routing product to export terminals. It processes an estimated 7% of global oil supply from one location.

The September 2019 drone and cruise missile attack on Abqaiq temporarily knocked out approximately 5.7 million barrels per day of production and demonstrated that physical vulnerability is real, not theoretical. Saudi Arabia restored capacity within weeks in 2019. The context in April 2026 is materially different: Hormuz is blocked by both Iranian mines and a US naval blockade, tankers are rerouting to a Yanbu terminal that itself was just degraded by an attack, and Iranian forces have now demonstrated the willingness to strike Saudi energy infrastructure repeatedly and at scale.

The April 8 strikes on Manifa, Khurais, Ras Tanura refinery, SATORP, SAMREF and the EW pipeline pumping station represent the most extensive simultaneous attack on Saudi energy infrastructure since 2019 — and came in a single coordinated wave.
